   Correct Answer: C
   Why: The correct CRM action is to recognise that fatigue (Dirty Dozen factor 6) is degrading your performance,
   which leads to lack of awareness (factor 11). Continuing alone increases the risk of missing an item. CRM
   principle: mutual support. Ask a colleague — perhaps the SCCM or another crew member — to cross-check your
   station. Reporting fatigue honestly is not a failure; it is the professional behaviour CRM demands. Safety is always
   more important than appearing capable.



2.11 Simplified Case Study
   CASE STUDY
   The Importance of Cross-Checking (anonymised)
   What Happened:
   On a busy evening departure, a cabin crew member completed the cabin secure check and reported to the SCCM
   that "cabin is secure." After take-off, a passenger in seat 22C noticed that the overwing exit was slightly ajar from
   inside — the cover had not been properly seated during the walk-around. The passenger alerted the crew, who
   reported to the cockpit. The flight continued safely, but the incident was reported and investigated.
   Why It Happened:
   The cabin crew member was distracted during the check by a passenger question and skipped the overwing exit
   verification step. The SCCM did not perform a separate cross-check, trusting the report at face value. The Swiss
   Cheese Model was at work: distraction (Dirty Dozen #4) created a hole in the cabin crew slice; lack of cross-check
   created a hole in the SCCM slice; the holes aligned and the error passed through to flight operations.
   CRM Lessons:
   Cross-checking is not a sign of distrust — it is a Swiss Cheese defence. Every safety-critical report should be
   verified by a second person when possible. Distraction is one of the most common Dirty Dozen factors in cabin
   operations; recognising it and managing it is a core CRM skill.
   How It Could Have Been Prevented:
   Train cabin crew to politely defer passenger questions during critical checks ("I will be with you in one moment")
   and to restart the check from the beginning if interrupted. SCCMs should perform independent cross-checks of
   safety-critical items rather than relying solely on verbal reports.

3.1 Why Communication Matters
Imagine you are meeting a friend at a busy train station. You call them and say "I am at the station." They reply
"OK, see you soon." You wait at the main entrance for twenty minutes. They wait at platform 4. Both of you did
your part — you spoke, they heard — but the communication failed. Why? Because the message was
incomplete, the context was not established, and there was no confirmation. You both assumed, and assumption
is the enemy of communication.
In aviation, communication failures are not measured in wasted minutes at a train station. They are measured in
lives. The Tenerife disaster of 1977 — the deadliest accident in aviation history — was fundamentally a
communication failure. The captain believed he had clearance to take off. The air traffic controller believed the
captain was holding position. Both spoke; neither truly understood the other. A clear, closed-loop exchange of
fewer than ten words would have prevented 583 deaths.
For cabin crew, communication is the single most important CRM skill. You will spend your entire working day
communicating: with passengers, with fellow cabin crew, with the SCCM, with the cockpit, and occasionally
with ground staff. Every safety procedure depends on communication. Every service interaction depends on
communication. Every emergency response depends on communication. A cabin crew member who is
technically excellent but communicates poorly is, in CRM terms, a safety risk. Conversely, a cabin crew
member who communicates clearly can compensate for many other limitations.

  REMEMBER
  Communication is not the same as speaking. Speaking is sending words. Communication is ensuring those words are
  received, understood, and acted upon. The sender is responsible for the entire cycle, not just the speaking part.



3.2 Types of Communication
Communication takes many forms. As cabin crew, you will use all of them every working day. Understanding
the strengths and limitations of each form helps you choose the right one for each situation.
          Type               Description                  Cabin Crew Example                   Strength / Limitation

 Verbal                Spoken words                  "Cabin crew, secure for departure"   Direct, fast / can be misheard

 Non-verbal            Gestures, eye contact,        Hand signal to colleague across      Fast, silent / can be misread
                       posture                       cabin

 Written               Text, notes, manuals, signs   Cabin log entry, safety card         Permanent, referenceable / slow

 Paralinguistic        Tone, pitch, speed of voice   Calm tone during emergency PA        Sets emotional tone / hard to control

 Visual                Diagrams, lights, displays    Fasten seatbelt sign, exit signs     Universal / requires line of sight

 Body language         Facial expression, posture    Smiling welcome during boarding      Builds rapport / culture-dependent

A critical insight: research suggests that in face-to-face communication, only about 7 percent of meaning is
conveyed by the words themselves. Tone of voice contributes roughly 38 percent, and body language around 55
percent. This means that what you say matters less than how you say it. A cabin crew member who says
"Welcome aboard" with a warm smile and open posture communicates welcome. The same words said with a
flat tone and turned-away body communicate indifference. In an emergency, calm tone and confident posture
communicate authority and reassure passengers far more effectively than the words alone.

3.3 The Communication Cycle
Every act of communication follows a predictable cycle. Understanding this cycle helps you diagnose where
communication breaks down — and where you can repair it.




           Figure 3.1 — The Communication Cycle. Effective communication requires all four stages to be completed.

1. Sender forms the message. You decide what you want to communicate. This sounds simple, but unclear
thinking produces unclear messages. Before you speak, ask yourself: what exactly do I want the other person to
know or do?
2. Encoding. You translate your thought into words, gestures, or writing. The choice of words, tone, and
channel all affect how the message will be received. "Cabin crew, prepare for departure" is clearer than "Get
ready, guys."
3. Receiver decodes. The receiver hears or sees the message and interprets it through their own filters —
language, culture, fatigue, attention, prior expectations. Decoding is where most communication failures occur.
4. Feedback. The receiver responds, confirming whether the message was understood as intended. Without
feedback, the cycle is open — the sender cannot know whether communication succeeded.

  WARNING
  An open communication loop — where feedback is missing — is the most common cause of misunderstanding in
  cabin operations. Never assume your message was understood. Always require confirmation, especially for
  safety-critical instructions.



3.4 Closed-Loop Communication
In aviation, we do not trust open loops. We require closed-loop communication, in which every safety-critical
message is repeated back by the receiver to confirm understanding. This practice, borrowed from air traffic
control and military operations, is the gold standard for cockpit-cabin communication and is taught in every
CRM course worldwide.




    Figure 3.2 — Closed-loop communication. The sender transmits, the receiver reads back, and the sender confirms.

The structure is simple: Sender transmits the message. Receiver repeats the message back in their own words
(called a readback). Sender confirms the readback is correct. Only when all three steps are complete is the loop
closed. If the readback is wrong, the sender corrects and the cycle repeats. This adds perhaps two seconds to a
message — and prevents misunderstandings that could cost lives.
        Step                               Phrase Example                                          Purpose

 1. Transmit           "Cabin crew, secure cabin for turbulence, aft galley"      Send clear instruction

 2. Readback           "Securing cabin for turbulence, aft galley, confirming"    Verify understanding

 3. Confirm            "Thank you, aft galley secured"                            Close the loop


  BEST PRACTICE
  The readback is not a parrot repetition. The receiver should rephrase in their own words to demonstrate genuine
  understanding. "Copy" or "Roger" alone do not close the loop for safety-critical messages — they only confirm
  receipt, not understanding.



3.5 Cabin Communication Channels
Cabin crew use several distinct communication channels during a flight. Each has its own protocol, advantages,
and limitations.
Cabin-to-cabin. Direct face-to-face conversation between cabin crew members. Used for routine coordination,
service tasks, and brief updates. Most common channel in everyday operations.
Cabin-to-cockpit. Communication between cabin crew (typically the SCCM) and the flight crew. Uses the
cabin interphone system. Must be brief, structured, and use standard phraseology. Critical for safety — never
interrupt the flight crew during high workload phases (take-off, landing, emergency) unless cabin safety
requires it.
Public Address (PA). Announcements to all passengers through the cabin speaker system. Used for routine
information (boarding, safety demo, descent) and emergency instructions. Must be clear, calm, and use standard
phraseology. In an emergency, the PA is your primary tool for directing passenger behaviour.
Passenger call bells. Lights and chimes indicating that a passenger requires attention. Cabin crew should
respond promptly, even if only to acknowledge and explain a delay. Ignored call bells signal poor service and
erode passenger confidence.
Crew call bells / interphone. Internal communication between cabin stations. Used for coordination without
walking the length of the cabin. Standard phraseology is essential — the interphone has no body language.

  DID YOU KNOW?
  The cabin interphone is a notoriously error-prone channel because it removes body language and tone cues. A simple
  phrase like "OK" can be interpreted as "I understand," "I agree," or "I will do that." Always use full, unambiguous
  sentences on the interphone. "OK, I will secure the aft cabin now" is far safer than "OK."

3.7 Passenger Communication
Passengers are not trained in aviation communication. They may be anxious, tired, drunk, frightened, confused,
or simply rude. Your communication with them must therefore be deliberate, professional, and adapted to the
situation. The same message may need to be delivered differently to a calm business traveller, a frightened
first-time flyer, a parent with three children, and an elderly passenger who does not hear well.
A useful framework is the 3 Cs of passenger communication: Calm, Clear, Confident. Calm tones defuse
anxiety. Clear language avoids ambiguity. Confident delivery builds trust. In an emergency, the 3 Cs become
life-saving: a panicked passenger hearing a calm, clear, confident instruction is far more likely to comply with
evacuation commands.

  BEST PRACTICE
  When communicating with a passenger who does not understand English, slow down rather than raise your voice. Use
  short sentences. Point to safety card pictures. Use universally understood gestures. Avoid aviation jargon. A passenger
  who understands is a passenger who will comply — and who may help others comply.
3.8 Emergency Communication
In an emergency, communication becomes both more important and more difficult. Noise increases. Stress
rises. Passengers may panic. Standard phraseology, drilled during training, becomes your lifeline. The words
you practiced in the classroom must come automatically, without conscious thought, even when your heart is
racing.
Modern airlines use standard emergency commands that are short, direct, and unambiguous. Examples include
"Brace, brace, brace!" (passengers adopt protective position), "Stay seated, stay seated!" (prevent premature
evacuation), "Open seatbelt, leave everything, come this way!" (evacuation commands). The exact phraseology
varies by airline, but the principle is universal: in an emergency, you do not improvise. You use the drilled
commands.

  WARNING
  In an emergency, never invent instructions. Use the airline-approved commands exactly as trained. Improvised words
  create hesitation; hesitation costs lives. If you are unsure of the correct command, use the standard command you
  remember — consistency is more important than perfect recall under stress.



3.9 Cross-Cultural Communication
Cabin crew serve passengers from every culture on Earth. What is polite in one culture may be rude in another.
Eye contact, personal space, gestures, and the use of first names all carry different meanings across cultures.
CRM training develops cultural awareness — not as political correctness, but as operational effectiveness. A
cabin crew member who offends a passenger through cultural ignorance creates a service failure and, in the
worst case, a safety failure if that passenger later refuses to cooperate during an emergency.
Practical guidelines: avoid gestures that may be offensive in some cultures (the "thumbs up" is rude in parts of
the Middle East; the "OK" circle is offensive in parts of South America). Use formal address (Sir, Madam) until
invited otherwise. Be patient with passengers whose first language is not English. Recognise that some cultures
communicate more indirectly than others — a "yes" may mean "I hear you" rather than "I agree." When in
doubt, ask clarifying questions and observe the passenger's response.

3.10 Real Cabin Crew Scenario
  REAL CABIN CREW SCENARIO
  During the pre-landing cabin secure check, the SCCM calls you on the interphone: "Cabin crew aft, confirm cabin
  secure for landing." You are still completing the aft galley check. You need about 90 more seconds.
  Question: What is the correct CRM response?
    A. "OK" — you will finish soon.
    B. "Stand by" — and continue the check.
    C. "Negative, aft cabin not yet secure, ninety seconds required." Then complete the check and call back with the
    readback.
    D. "Cabin secure" — you will catch up before landing.
  Correct Answer: C
  Why: The correct response follows closed-loop communication principles. "OK" is ambiguous — it could mean "I
  understand" or "cabin is secure." "Stand by" is better but does not give the SCCM information they need. Reporting
  "negative, not yet secure, ninety seconds required" gives the SCCM accurate information to relay to the cockpit,
  who can then decide whether to delay landing. Saying "cabin secure" when it is not is a critical safety failure. CRM
  principle: closed-loop, honest, specific communication.



3.11 Simplified Case Study

  CASE STUDY
  The Cost of an Open Loop (anonymised, instructive)
  What Happened:
  During boarding on a busy evening flight, the SCCM asked a junior cabin crew member over the interphone: "Are
  we ready for closing doors?" The junior replied "OK." The SCCM interpreted "OK" as "yes, ready," and signalled
  the ground staff to close the doors and disconnect the jet bridge. In fact, the junior meant "OK, I heard you, but I am
  still counting passengers in the aft cabin." The doors closed, the aircraft pushed back, and a passenger count
  discrepancy was only discovered during the climb. The aircraft returned to the gate.
  Why It Happened:
  The interphone channel removed body language and tone cues. "OK" was ambiguous. The SCCM did not require a
  clear readback. The communication loop was open — sender assumed, receiver assumed, neither confirmed. A
  Swiss Cheese alignment: ambiguous phraseology + open loop + time pressure.
  CRM Lessons:
  Closed-loop communication is non-negotiable for safety-critical messages. "OK" is never an acceptable readback
  for a safety question. The correct exchange would have been: "Aft cabin, are we ready for door closure?" —
  "Negative, still counting aft cabin, two minutes required." — "Copy, two minutes, standing by." The loop would
  have closed safely.
  How It Could Have Been Prevented:
  Train all cabin crew to refuse ambiguous readbacks. When you receive "OK" to a safety question, ask explicitly:
  "Confirm ready — yes or no?" Standard phraseology, drilled in initial training and reinforced in every recurrent
  session, prevents this type of failure.
